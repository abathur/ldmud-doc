2  /**
3  * The function strftime() formats the given time in <clock> as a string,
4  * similar to ctime(). Unlike ctime(), strftime() accepts a format string
5  * with placeholders for the different elements. The format string
6  * consists of zero or more conversion specifiers (see below) and ordinary
7  * characters. Ordinary characters are just copied to the result. A
8  * conversion specifier starts with a percent sign ('%'). If no format
9  * string is given, a default of "%c" will be used.
10  *
11  * The argument <clock> is the number of seconds since the epoch (00:00:00
12  * UTC, January 1, 1970), as returned by time/mktime(). If <clock> is not
13  * given, the current result of time() will be used.
14  *
15  * The argument <localized> specifies whether the result will be in English
16  * (the classic "C" locale) or in the language configured on the host
17  * computer (e.g. German). The language has to be configured with the
18  * environment variables LC_TIME or LC_ALL (please ask your admins). If
19  * <localized> is not given, the default is 1.
20  * 0: use classic "C" locale (english)
21  * 1: use current locale on the host computer (national representation).
22  *
23  * @synopsis{
24  * string strftime()
25  * string strftime(string fmt)
26  * string strftime(int clock)
27  * string strftime(string fmt, int clock)
28  * string strftime(string fmt, int clock, int localized)
29  * }
30  *
31  **/
32  string strftime(string fmt, int clock, int localized);
33 }
34 REMARKS:
35  The resulting string is at most 511 character long.
36 
37 CONVERSION SPECIFIERS:
38  This function accepts all conversion specifiers which the function
39  strftime() from the C library accepts. Currently these are:
40 
41  %A replaced by national representation of the full weekday name.
42  %a replaced by national representation of the abbreviated weekday
43 
44  name.
45 
46  %B replaced by national representation of the full month name.
47  %b replaced by national representation of the abbreviated month name.
48  %C replaced by (year / 100) as decimal number; single digits are
49 
50  preceded by a zero.
51 
52  %c replaced by national representation of time and date.
53  %D equivalent to "%m/%d/%y".
54  %d replaced by the day of the month as a decimal number (01-31).
55  %E* %O*
56 
57  POSIX locale extensions. The sequences %Ec %EC %Ex %EX %Ey %EY %Od
58  %Oe %OH %OI %Om %OM %OS %Ou %OU %OV %Ow %OW %Oy are supposed to
59  provide alternate representations.
60  Additionally %OB implemented to represent alternative
61  month names (used standalone, without day mentioned).
62 
63  %e replaced by the day of month as a decimal number (1-31); single
64  digits are preceded by a blank.
65 
66  %F equivalent to "%Y-%m-%d".
67  %G replaced by a year as a decimal number with century. This year
68 
69  is the one that contains the greater part of the week (Monday as
70  the first day of the week).
71 
72  %g replaced by the same year as in "%G", but as a decimal number
73  without century (00-99).
74 
75  %H replaced by the hour (24-hour clock) as a decimal number (00-23).
76  %h the same as %b.
77  %I replaced by the hour (12-hour clock) as a decimal number (01-12).
78  %j replaced by the day of the year as a decimal number (001-366).
79  %k replaced by the hour (24-hour clock) as a decimal number (0-23);
80 
81  single digits are preceded by a blank.
82 
83  %l replaced by the hour (12-hour clock) as a decimal number (1-12);
84  single digits are preceded by a blank.
85 
86  %M replaced by the minute as a decimal number (00-59).
87  %m replaced by the month as a decimal number (01-12).
88  %n replaced by a newline.
89  %O* the same as %E*.
90  %p replaced by national representation of either "ante meridiem" or
91 
92  "post meridiem" as appropriate.
93 
94  %R equivalent to "%H:%M".
95  %r equivalent to "%I:%M:%S %p".
96  %S replaced by the second as a decimal number (00-60).
97  %s replaced by the number of seconds since the Epoch, UTC (see
98 
99  mktime(3)).
100 
101  %T equivalent to "%H:%M:%S".
102  %t replaced by a tab.
103  %U replaced by the week number of the year (Sunday as the
104 
105  first day of the week) as a decimal number (00-53).
106 
107  %u replaced by the weekday (Monday as the first day of
108  the week) as a decimal number (1-7).
109 
110  %V replaced by the week number of the year (Monday as the
111  first day of the week) as a decimal number (01-53). If the week
112  containing January 1st has four or more days in the new year,
113  then it is week 1; otherwise it is the last week of the previous
114  year, and the next week is week 1.
115 
116  %v equivalent to "%e-%b-%Y".
117  %W replaced by the week number of the year (Monday as the
118 
119  first day of the week) as a decimal number (00-53).
120 
121  %w replaced by the weekday (Sunday as the first day of the week)
122  as a decimal number (0-6).
123 
124  %X replaced by national representation of the time.
125  %x replaced by national representation of the date.
126  %Y replaced by the year with century as a decimal number.
127  %y replaced by the year without century as a decimal number (00-99).
128  %Z replaced by the time zone name.
129  %z replaced by the time zone offset from UTC; a leading plus sign
130 
131  stands for east of UTC, a minus sign for west of UTC, hours and
132  minutes follow with two digits each and no delimiter between
133  them (common form for RFC 822 date headers).
134 
135  %+ replaced by national representation of the date and time
136  (the format is similar to that produced by date(1)).
137 
138  %% replaced by >>`<<%'.
